# Combination Products (Drug-Device)

## What is a combination product?
A **combination product** is a product that combines a medical device component and a drug or biologic component in a single integral product. Examples include drug-eluting stents, pre-filled syringes, antimicrobial catheters, and insulin pens with integrated drug cartridges.

## Regulatory determination
Health Canada determines the primary regulatory pathway based on the **primary mode of action (PMOA)**:
- If the PMOA is through the **drug/biologic** component → regulated as a drug (device component reviewed as part of drug submission)
- If the PMOA is through the **device** component → regulated as a medical device (drug component reviewed as part of device application)

## Device-led combination products
For combination products regulated as medical devices, the Device Licence application must address both the device and drug components:
- Device safety and effectiveness per Schedule 1
- Drug/substance safety — toxicology, pharmacology, stability
- Interaction between device and drug components
- Shelf life and sterilisation compatibility

## Seeking a regulatory determination
For genuinely ambiguous combination products, manufacturers should contact Health Canada's Medical Devices Directorate for a **product classification determination** before beginning the regulatory process.

## Co-packaged products (non-integral)
Products where a device and drug are co-packaged but not physically or chemically combined (e.g., a device kit that includes a drug for use with the device) are generally regulated as separate products:
- The device is licensed as a device
- The drug is approved as a drug
- The combined labelling and IFU must address the use of both together

> **Legislative source:** Food and Drugs Act, s 2 (definitions); Medical Devices Regulations, SOR/98-282

For device-led combination products, manufacturers must provide stability data demonstrating that the device component does not chemically or physically degrade the drug component, and vice versa. This includes data across the claimed shelf life, storage conditions, and after sterilisation (if applicable). Compatibility testing protocols should be aligned with both drug and device guidance documents.
