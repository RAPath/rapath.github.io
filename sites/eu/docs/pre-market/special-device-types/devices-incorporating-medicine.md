# Devices incorporating a medicinal substance

:::note Regulatory basis
**MDR Annex VIII Rule 14** (classification); **MDR Art. 1(8)–(9)** (drug-device combination products); **MDR Annex I §8** (GSPR). Integral drug-device combinations where the device action is principal are **Class III** under MDR and require EMA or NCA consultation.
:::

:::warning Disclaimer
This site provides general information only and does not constitute legal or regulatory advice. Drug-device combination products involve complex dual-framework interactions — always seek specialist regulatory advice.
:::

---

## The two types of drug-device combinations

EU law distinguishes between two fundamentally different drug-device scenarios:

### Type 1 — Integral combination, device action principal (MDR Art. 1(8))

A single product where the device and drug cannot be separated, and the **device mechanism is the primary action**. The drug plays an **ancillary role** — assisting or enhancing the device function.

Regulatory path: **MDR governs the whole product** → automatically **Class III** under Rule 14.

Examples:
- Drug-eluting coronary stents (heparin or sirolimus coating — drug is ancillary to mechanical scaffolding)
- Antibiotic-impregnated bone cement (antibiotic is ancillary to structural function)
- Chlorhexidine-coated central venous catheters
- Silver-releasing wound dressings (where the silver is integral and not separable)

### Type 2 — Integral combination, drug action principal (MDR Art. 1(9))

A single product where the drug mechanism is the primary action. The device component is **ancillary** to the drug delivery.

Regulatory path: **Medicinal product legislation governs** → EMA or NCA assesses the whole product. The device component must still meet relevant MDR GSPRs.

Examples:
- Pre-filled syringes or auto-injectors containing a proprietary medicinal product (the device component — syringe/needle/autoinjector — is ancillary to the drug)
- Metered-dose inhalers where the pharmaceutical formulation is the principal element

### Type 3 — Co-packaged device and drug (separate products)

A device and a drug packaged together but **not integral** — they can be used separately. Each product is regulated independently:
- The device under MDR
- The drug under Directive 2001/83/EC or Regulation 726/2004

Examples: a kit containing a syringe (Class I device) and a separately authorised injectable medicine.

## Class III classification under Rule 14

MDR Annex VIII Rule 14 provides:

> All devices that incorporate, as an integral part, a substance which, if used separately, would be considered a medicinal product under Directive 2001/83/EC, including a medicinal product derived from human blood or plasma, and that has action ancillary to that of the device, are in **Class III**.

Key elements:
- The substance must be **integral** (cannot be separated and used independently)
- The substance must be a **medicinal product** if used alone
- The substance's action must be **ancillary** to the device's principal action
- If these conditions are met: **automatically Class III** regardless of what other rules might suggest

## EMA/NCA consultation requirement

For devices that are integral drug-device combinations under Art. 1(8) (device-led), before the notified body can issue a certificate, it must:

1. Assess the quality, safety, and usefulness of the **medicinal substance** component
2. Seek a **scientific opinion** from either:
   - **EMA** (European Medicines Agency) — for substances subject to centralised authorisation procedure
   - The **national competent authority** of a member state — for other substances

The EMA/NCA has 210 days to issue an opinion. The notified body must then:
- Take the opinion into account
- Document any departure from the opinion with justification

This consultation is **mandatory** and can significantly extend the certification timeline. Manufacturers should engage with EMA/NCA **early in development** — informal scientific advice meetings are available.

---

## GSPR §8 — Technical requirements for the medicinal component

Annex I §8 requires that for devices incorporating a medicinal substance:
- The substance's safety, quality, and usefulness must be verified having regard to the device's intended purpose
- The substance's bioavailability, local concentration, and systemic exposure must be characterised
- Analytical methods for controlling the substance in the finished device must be validated
- Stability of the substance in the device must be demonstrated throughout the shelf life
- Where the substance is a human blood derivative: additional requirements of Directive 2002/98/EC apply

---

## Practical regulatory timeline

Drug-device combination products under Art. 1(8) are among the most complex and lengthy regulatory pathways:

| Stage | Typical timeline |
|---|---|
| Pre-submission planning | 6–12 months before NB application |
| Notified body application and QMS/tech doc review | 12–24 months |
| EMA/NCA scientific opinion (Art. 1(8)) | Up to 210 days (from NB referral) |
| Final NB certificate decision | Additional 3–6 months after opinion |
| **Total (Class III combination product)** | **30–48+ months** |

Manufacturers should plan for this extended timeline and engage EMA/NCA and their notified body in parallel where possible.
