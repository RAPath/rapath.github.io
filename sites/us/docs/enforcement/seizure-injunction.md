# Seizure, Injunction & Criminal Prosecution

These are FDA's most powerful enforcement tools, used when voluntary compliance fails or when public health requires immediate action.

---

## 1. Seizure — FD&C Act § 304

**Seizure** allows FDA to physically remove violative goods (adulterated or misbranded devices) from commerce. FDA does not have independent seizure authority — it must work through the **Department of Justice (DOJ)** and the **US Marshals Service**.

### How seizure works

1. FDA identifies violative devices in commerce
2. FDA refers the matter to **DOJ with a recommendation for seizure**
3. DOJ files a civil action in federal district court (e.g., *United States v. [Quantity] [Device Name]*)
4. Court issues a warrant of arrest for the goods
5. US Marshals execute the seizure — physically taking possession of the goods
6. **Claimant** (typically the manufacturer) has 30 days to contest the seizure
7. If unchallenged, the goods are condemned and destroyed or recalled

### Grounds for seizure
- Device is **adulterated** (e.g., manufactured in violation of QMSR)
- Device is **misbranded** (e.g., inadequate labelling, false claims)
- Device is being offered for sale without required clearance/approval

---

## 2. Injunction — FD&C Act § 302

An **injunction** is a court order requiring a company to stop a violative activity (permanent injunction) or to take specific corrective actions. FDA seeks injunctions through DOJ.

### Types of injunctions

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Temporary restraining order (TRO)** | Immediate, short-term halt pending a hearing |
| **Preliminary injunction** | Halt during litigation |
| **Permanent injunction** | Long-term court order; often called a "consent decree" when the company agrees |

### Consequences of a permanent injunction
- Must **cease manufacturing** of violative devices
- Must implement FDA-approved **remediation plan**
- Must engage **FDA-approved independent expert** for oversight
- Must achieve expert certification before resuming manufacturing
- Subject to **civil penalties** for future violations

---

## 3. Criminal Prosecution — FD&C Act § 303

Criminal prosecution is reserved for the most serious violations — particularly wilful violations or cases involving serious patient harm.

### Standards for criminal liability

| Violation type | Standard | Maximum penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Misdemeanour (non-wilful) | No intent required — strict liability for responsible corporate officials | 1 year / fine |
| Felony (wilful) | Must prove the defendant knowingly committed the violation | 3 years / larger fine |

### The "responsible corporate officer" doctrine
Under the **Park doctrine** (US v. Park, 1975), corporate officers may be criminally liable for violations committed by their company even without personal knowledge of the violation — if they had authority to prevent or correct the violation. This applies to FDA device cases.
